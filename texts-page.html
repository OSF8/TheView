The DisruptorsYesColors

A conversation about a garage full of paint tins and the difficulties with disposing of them was the catalyst for the launch of forward-thinking new company YesColours, who have tackled the problem by developing Europe’s first waste saving, fully recyclable paint pouches.

“Currently over 55 million litres of paint and 14,000 tonnes of tins go to landfill every year and only two per cent are recycled, which is astonishing,” says creative director and co-founder Emma Bestley. “Our aim is to empower customers that care about the environment, and being hugely passionate about sustainability ourselves it’s at the heart of how we operate; from supply chains to raw materials, to supporting British manufacturers. Our paint is also available by the litre to help avoid over ordering, which is a massive contributor to UK paint waste.”

Formulating vegan, cruelty-free products with zero per cent added VOCs (Volatile organic compounds that give synthetic products a ‘new carpet’ smell and are sometimes harmful) was also a priority, as was avoiding the use of any microplastics.

“We wanted relatable colours that speak to people who are ready to express themselves creatively,” continues Bestley. “These shades are inspired by the world around us – culture, travel, nature, movies, music and even food. Our palette is less trend focused and more about personal connection to colour.”

Family run, independent and UK based, Little Greene have always offered an impressive palette of traditional and contemporary colours, while at the same time championing a sustainable approach, but their latest collection takes ecological innovation to the next level.

Using leftover and returned paints that are reformulated into an interior matt finish, ‘Re:mix’ is available in a selection of popular colours from their much-loved range.

“In the last couple of years we’ve really seen consumers looking for products that not only make them happy from a design perspective but that are ethically and sustainably produced, and upcycling in this way prevents as much as 60,000 litres of mineral and organic raw materials from going to waste each year,” explains creative director Ruth Mottershead.

“The process was complex and time-consuming, but it’s amazing being able to tackle the issue of unwanted stock while enabling customers to make sustainable and eco-friendly decorating choices,” she says. “There is always more to do, and we’ll continue to review our ways of working to further reduce our environmental impact, but it’s a fantastic project to bring to market and one which will hopefully inspire other manufacturers too.”

But what about the future? Working from home and the prospect of a four-day week appear not to have diminished Sir George’s optimism. He recalls conversations led by the group more than 20 years ago that catalysed the Crossrail, or Elizabeth Line, project. “Crossrail acts like an elastic,” he says. “Either it pulls the City towards Canary Wharf, or it pulls Canary Wharf towards the City and the West End. Canary Wharf will thrive. And in time, close your eyes, and Canary Wharf and the City will be one piece.”
